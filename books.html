Books

"Knowledge is Power"

Books are a powerful source of knowledge. Books allow you to travel across the world and across different periods in time without having to ever leave your chair. My favorite thing I've heard about books are that they allow you to have a one-on-one conversation with someone that you'd otherwise never get to speak with. Here are my top nine favorite books:

The Short and Tragic Life of Robert Pearce by Jeff Hobbs
  • Score: 95/100
  • Category: Biography
  • Summary:
    Hobbs tells the whirlwind story of Robert Pearce, his college roommate at Yale, a talented and highly intelligent young man who wasn’t able to escape the trappings of growing up in the temptations of the crime life from his younger days. Both inspiring and saddening at the same time, you root for Pearce even though you already know the ending.
The Obstacle Is the Way by Ryan Holiday
  • Score: 93/100
  • Category: Philosophy
  • Summary:
    As an ardent follower of Stoicism, Holiday distills wisdom from Ancient Greek philosophers and applies them to modern life, providing the reader with a valuable framework and lens to approach problems and challenges.
Predictably Irrational by Dan Ariely
  • Score: 93/100
  • Category: Behavioral Economics
  • Summary:
    Ariely adeptly distills, in approachable terms, many major findings of the academic research that he and other behavioral economists have done, pointing out the many ways that we, as humans, are irrational. Both entertaining and useful, this book is must-read for designers or anyone who is fascinated by the human mind (and its failings).
Buck by M.K. Asante
  • Score: 92/100
  • Category: Autobiography
  • Summary:
    Asante writes his autobiography like a novel. His writing style is amazing as he tells his story of growing up in Philadelphia, his family, his struggles, and how he came to fall in love with writing. His story left me wanting to know more, more about him as a man, his family, everything. Great book.
I Will Teach You to Be Rich by Ramit Sethi
  • Score: 90/100
  • Category: Personal Finance
  • Summary:
    Despite the absurd title, Sethi makes personal finance fun for the early 20s - early 30s crowd. Easy to understand and with actionable steps about how to save more, where and how to best invest, Seth’s book should be the manual for young people just starting on their personal finance journey.
Just Mercy by Bryan Stevenson
  • Score: 90/100
  • Category: Autobiography
  • Summary:
    Bryan Stevenson, a lawyer who started the Equal Justice Initiative, tells the story of how he started this organization to fight against wrongful convictions and harsh death penalties. One of the best books I read in 2017, he is truly doing meaningful work
Shoe Dog by Phil Knight
  • Score: 89/100
  • Category: Autobiography
  • Summary:
    One of the better business memoirs that I have read. Knight tells the story of the early days of Nike, sharing both the ups and downs. Very easy to read, the book is highly enjoyable. The best part for me was that he never really doles out advice, but instead says, "hey this was my life" and let's the reader take it from there.
Stick With It by Sean D. Young
  • Score: 88/100
  • Category: Psychology
  • Summary:
    A great first book by Young, a medical and psychological researcher at UCLA. he uses scientifically proven methods to change and maintain behaviors in yourself and others. Fascinating.
Insight Out by Tina Seelig
  • Score: 88/100
  • Category: Psychology
  • Summary:
    A Professor of Creativity and Innovation at Stanford, Seelig walks through exercises that she does to get creative ideas out of their heads and into the world. This is an actionable book that helps the creator in your to get business and design ideas from your brain into existence.
